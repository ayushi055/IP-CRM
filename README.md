# IP-CRM
Review of IP-CRM For Phase I Clinical Trials

## What is IP-CRM?
The method proposed by Guo and Liu is the Intra-Patient Continuous Reassessment Method (IP-CRM). This is an adaptive design for Phase I clinical trials which incorporates patients receiving multiple dose levels over the course of the trial, allowing for intra-patient dose escalation based on their tolerance to toxicity. The IP-CRM design offers several advantages over traditional methods like 3+3. Since it allows for a greater amount of data points with few patients, it makes more efficient use of the available data and may identify the optimal dose more precisely. Additionally, by updating the MTD based on real-time data, the method adapts to the observed toxicity profile, potentially improving patient safety and trial efficiency. However, the approach also comes with challenges, including the need for complex modeling and careful management of intra-patient correlations, which can make implementation more technically de-manding. This review will cover the strengths and limitations of the proposed IP-CRM along with presenting a comparison of this method to more traditional Phase I designs through simu-lation.

## Simulation Study
A simulation study was conducted to evaluate the performance of the IP-CRM in comparison to the standard 3+3 method and CRM in situations with a relatively small number of participants. The simulations were set up with the following parameters: 5 dose levels (J), a maximum of 9 patients (N), and a cohort size of 3 patients. The target toxicity rate was set to 0.3, representing the desired toxicity rate for identifying the MTD. Additionally, prior distributions were defined for the logistic regression parameters used in the Bayesian models of IP-CRM and CRM, with β_0  ~ N(0,1) and β_1  ~ Exp(1). A relatively uninformative distribution for the intercept term was chosen to avoid biasing the estimate for the dose effect, β_1. The prior distribution for the dose effect was chosen based on what was specified in the original article. A Gibbs sampling algorithm was used to sample from the posterior distribution of the model parameters using the ‘R2jags’ package in R. The maximum number of titrations for each patient was limited to 3, and an early termination toxicity threshold of 0.5 was applied for the IP-CRM method to stop the trial early if toxicity at the lowest dose level exceeded this threshold.

Four true toxicity scenarios were simulated, each representing different patterns of toxicity for the dose levels. In Scenario 1, the true MTD was at dose level 2 with increasing toxicity probabilities. Scenario 2 had the true MTD at dose level 3 and Scenario 3 had the MTD at dose level 4. Scenario 4 presented a non-monotonic increase in toxicity, with the true MTD at dose level 2 but with a less predictable pattern of toxicity across dose levels. Full descriptions of the true toxicity rates for each scenario are presented in Table 1. The simulation was conducted across 2000 trials for each of the four toxicity scenarios, and for each trial, the final MTD and safety index (the proportion of toxic patients) were recorded for each method.

## Results

In Scenario 1, where the true MTD was at dose level 2, IP-CRM selected dose 1 most frequent-ly (67.75%), followed by dose 2 (26.55%), with very few selections for higher doses. This indicates that IP-CRM tended to favor lower doses, even though dose 2 was the true MTD. CRM also selected dose 1 most often (59.55%) but also showed substantial selection of dose 2 (21.25%) and dose 3 (18.45%), indicating some variability and less precision. The 3+3 method selected dose 1 most frequently (49.50%) and dose 2 (29.20%), showing a greater degree of accuracy in selecting the true MTD, but it still over-selected dose 1 more than necessary.

In Scenario 2, where the true MTD was at dose level 3, IP-CRM selected dose 2 most frequently (57.30%), followed by dose 3 (25.90%), and dose 1 (12.25%). This indicates that IP-CRM underperformed and over-selected dose 2 compared to dose 3, the true MTD. CRM selected dose 3 most often (41.00%), but it also showed notable selection of doses 1 (33.35%) and 2 (23.30%), showing more accuracy and less precision. The 3+3 method selected dose 3 most frequently (39.60%), followed by doses 1 (20.85%) and 2 (21.60%), performing better than IP-CRM but with high variability.

In Scenario 3, where the true MTD was at dose level 4, IP-CRM selected dose 3 most frequently (33.40%), followed by dose 4 (25.65%), with dose 5 being selected 31.50% of the time. This suggests that IP-CRM performed well by selecting doses 3 and 4 near the true MTD, although it over-selected dose 5. CRM selected dose 3 most frequently (72.35%), mak-ing it less accurate in identifying the true MTD. The 3+3 method selected dose 4 most frequent-ly (58.95%), followed by dose 3 (30.75%), showing the best accuracy.

In Scenario 4, where the true MTD was at dose level 2, IP-CRM selected dose 2 most frequently (39.10%), followed by dose 1 (19.65%) and dose 3 (25.85%). This suggests that IP-CRM performed reasonably well in identifying the true MTD in a case where the toxicity probability was non-monotonically increasing, which violated the model assumptions, but also showed some selection of lower doses. CRM selected dose 1 most often (38.80%), followed by dose 2 (33.10%), showing less accuracy in targeting the true MTD. The 3+3 method select-ed dose 1 most frequently (41.10%), with dose 2 selected 24.40% of the time, indicating less accuracy compared to IP-CRM.

The safety index, which represents the proportion of patients who exhibited toxicity, varied across the different methods and scenarios. The 3+3 method generally had a moderate safety index, with values ranging from 0.064 in Scenario 3 to 0.269 in Scenario 1. This indicat-ed a higher toxicity rate in scenarios where lower doses were more frequently selected. The CRM method consistently had the lowest safety index, ranging from 0.028 in Scenario 3 to 0.132 in Scenario 1, suggesting that it resulted in fewer toxicities overall. The IP-CRM method had the highest safety index across all scenarios, with values ranging from 0.201 in Scenario 3 to 0.346 in Scenario 1, indicating a higher proportion of patients experiencing toxicity due to its selection of higher doses.

## Discussion
One of the primary strengths of the IP-CRM method is its ability to potentially reduce the sam-ple size required to determine the MTD. Since each patient contributes multiple data points through intra-patient dose escalation, fewer patients are needed compared to traditional dose-finding designs, such as the 3+3 method. This is particularly advantageous in early-phase trials where sample size is often limited due to time and ethical constraints. The early termination rule, which halts the trial if the lowest dose is found to be too toxic, further improves patient safety. Furthermore, the use of posterior mean estimation for toxicity probability offers a more statisti-cally informed and precise determination of the MTD, making it more reliable than traditional methods like 3+3 that rely on arbitrary thresholds.

Based on the simulation results, it is difficult to determine which method performed the best because each method had strengths and weaknesses in different scenarios. IP-CRM gener-ally showed a good balance by selecting doses closer to the true MTD in most scenarios, but it tended to over-select lower doses, such as in Scenario 1 where it favored dose 1 even though dose 2 was the true MTD. CRM, on the other hand, showed more variability, with increased accuracy in certain scenarios but also substantial mis-selections, such as over-selecting dose 1 in Scenario 4, where the true MTD was at dose 2. The 3+3 method showed the least variability in some scenarios, like Scenario 3, where it selected dose 4 most frequently, but it still strug-gled with over-selection of lower doses, particularly in Scenarios 1 and 4. 

Due to the nature of intra-patient dose escalation, the IP-CRM method had the highest proportion of observed toxicities as patients who observed no toxicity continued to have their dosage escalated, resulting in a higher probability of patients receiving higher doses in this method compared to the other two. IP-CRM, like other Bayesian methods, is sensitive to the prior assumptions made about the toxicity probability distribution. If those priors are mis-matched to the actual data or are overly restrictive, it could lead to less flexibility in the dose-finding process, making it difficult for the model to adapt properly to the observed toxicity rates. It is likely in this case that the model was a poor fit due to the chosen priors for the pa-rameters. In real-world scenarios, more complex sampling procedures would have to be em-ployed to better estimate and refine the priors. These more complex sampling procedures can help the model adapt to the observed data more effectively and allow for greater flexibility in the dose-finding process.

The limitations of the IP-CRM method were highlighted in the simulation. One key issue is that the method does not account for intra-patient correlation, which could lead to biased es-timates if patients' responses to treatment are not independent. This limitation is particularly concerning in real-world applications where individual patients may not have similar responses to treatment, and including a random effects term to model patient-specific variability could im-prove the method's accuracy. However, incorporating such terms could complicate the model and may be difficult to estimate with small sample sizes. Additionally, the method's sensitivity to prior assumptions about toxicity probabilities is a notable challenge, especially due to the small sample size. If the chosen priors are poorly matched to the actual data, the model may be too restrictive and unable to adapt properly to observed toxicity rates. This sensitivity makes the method less flexible in certain scenarios, which could lead to less accurate MTD estimates. In real-world settings, more complex sampling procedures might be required to better estimate and refine these priors, allowing the model to adapt more effectively. Finally, when implementing an IP-CRM design it is important to carefully select the maximum number of titrations a patient can receive in order to avoid excess toxicity. As shown in the simulation, IP-CRM can result in increased amounts of observed toxicities compared to other methods. The dosage at each level and other parameters should be specified after taking these issues into account. 

Despite the results observed in this simulation study, Bayesian approaches, including methods like CRM, have been shown to consistently outperform the traditional 3+3 design in several cases [8]. In real-world scenarios, more research is needed to refine these types of mod-els, particularly with respect to incorporating patient-specific variability and improving prior estimation techniques to ensure that the method provides reliable and generalizable results.
